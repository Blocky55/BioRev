## Topic 1 — DNA and the Human Genome Project
**Lecturer:** Dr Maggy Fostier

### Part A: Human DNA Basics

- Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs): 22 pairs of autosomes and 1 pair of sex chromosomes.
- A karyotype shows these 23 pairs when a cell begins dividing.
- DNA is the hereditary material. Each chromosome is one DNA molecule packed tightly.
- **Mitosis** = cell duplication. Makes an exact copy of all 46 chromosomes, passed to daughter cells.
- **Meiosis** = formation of sex cells (gametes). Each gamete has only 23 chromosomes (one from each pair).
- DNA = a double helix with two strands held together by hydrogen bonds between base pairs: A-T and C-G.
- **Semi-conservative replication**: before dividing, a cell duplicates each DNA molecule using base pair complementarity.
- Genes code for proteins. Transcription produces RNA from DNA; translation produces protein from RNA. Uracil replaces thymine in RNA.
- The "restaurant analogy": genome = menu, RNA = orders, proteins = dishes.

### Alleles and Polymorphisms

- **Allele**: any of several forms of a gene, arising through mutation, responsible for hereditary variation.
- **Polymorphism**: existence of several alleles for one gene locus.
- Individuals have two alleles per locus (one from each parent). They may be the same (homozygous) or different (heterozygous).
- **Dominant allele**: masks the presence of the other allele. One copy is sufficient to express the trait.
- **Recessive allele**: an organism needs two copies to express the associated trait.
- **Co-dominant alleles**: both are expressed simultaneously (e.g. AB blood group).
- Allele relationships reflect the relative characteristics or abundance of the proteins they encode.

### Genotype and Phenotype

- **Genotype**: the genetic makeup of an individual — the combination of alleles for one or more genes.
- **Phenotype**: the observable characteristics of an individual — morphology, physiology, or behaviour.
- A trait (phenotype) is controlled by one or more proteins (genotype), and sometimes the environment.
- The environment can influence physiology, switch genes ON or OFF (e.g. stress, nutrients), and shape natural selection.

### Natural Selection

- Allele frequency is linked to the genetic fitness it confers in a given environment.
- Genetic fitness = reproductive success: survival to reproductive age + mating success + healthy offspring.
- If an allele provides an advantage, it persists or becomes more common. If the environment changes, frequency may shift again (micro-evolution).
- **Examples of beneficial mutations**:
  - TAS2R38 mutation: reduced bitter taste receptor function, allowed eating previously bitter vegetables.
  - Lactase persistence: mutation maintaining lactase production into adulthood, fitted with cattle farming 10,000 years ago.
  - Sickle cell allele: heterozygous carriers are protected from malaria; homozygous individuals develop sickle cell anaemia.
  - CCR5 mutation: homozygous individuals are immune to HIV; thought to have previously protected against plague and smallpox.
- Environment can affect phenotype directly: temperature-sensitive alleles (e.g. Siamese cats — tyrosinase is inactive at higher temperatures, so melanin only produced in cool extremities), diet and nutrients (e.g. flamingo pink colouration depends on dietary carotene).

### Multifactorial Traits

- Most phenotypes are due to several genes plus the environment.
- Examples: skin colour, height, weight, intelligence, behaviour.
- These phenotypes exist on a continuum.
- **Twin studies**: identical twins share 100% of DNA; fraternal twins share ~50%. Greater similarity between identical twins for a trait suggests genetic involvement. Less than 100% concordance in identical twins indicates the environment also plays a role.

### Part B: Mutations and the Human Genome Project

### Types of Mutation

- **Mutation**: a permanent alteration in the DNA sequence, passed on to daughter cells and sometimes gametes.
- **Hereditary (germline) mutation**: inherited from a parent gamete; present in every cell of the body.
- **Acquired (somatic) mutation**: occurs during a person's life; present only in cells where it occurred and their daughters.

**Causes of new mutations:**
- Environmental mutagens (e.g. radiation): cause breaks or intercalation between DNA bases.
- Biological factors (e.g. viruses): integrate into the genome and disrupt important regions.
- Errors during DNA replication before mitosis.
- Errors during DNA repair.
- Errors during meiosis (e.g. incorrect chromosome separation → Trisomy 21 / Down syndrome).

**Macro mutations (chromosomal):**
- Numerical aberrations (e.g. extra chromosome: Down syndrome = trisomy 21; Klinefelter syndrome = XXY).
- Structural aberrations (e.g. deletion: Cri du chat = deletion on chromosome 5).
- Occur during meiosis or in late-stage cancer.

**Micro mutations — SNPs:**
- Single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP, pronounced "snip") = single base substitution.
- Can produce: silent mutations (no change in amino acid), nonsense mutations (early STOP codon), or missense mutations (wrong amino acid).
- The third letter of a codon is less discriminating (wobble position).

**Micro mutations — insertions and deletions:**
- Insertions and deletions can cause **frameshift mutations** (unless the number of bases is a multiple of 3).
- Frameshift mutations usually lead to an early STOP codon → shorter or absent protein → usually very harmful.

**The importance of mutation location:**
- Mutations in exons directly affect the protein sequence.
- Mutations in promoters or enhancer elements affect when, where, and how much of a protein is made.
- Mutations in introns are often harmless.

**Key disease examples:**
- **Cystic Fibrosis (CF)**: caused by mutations in the CFTR gene. Over 900 'bad' alleles exist, all recessive against the normal allele. CF patients are homozygous or compound heterozygous for bad alleles.
- **Trinucleotide repeat expansions**: CAG repeats code for glutamine. When the polyglutamine stretch becomes too long, proteins aggregate. Examples: Huntington's disease.
- **Lactase persistence SNP**: a C→T SNP at position -13910 upstream of the LCT gene allows Oct-1 transcription factor to bind, activating lactase production into adulthood. The T allele is dominant.

### The Human Genome Project (HGP)

- First complete draft published 2004. Took 13 years. Sequenced 3 billion base pairs from 5 anonymous individuals of varying ethnicity.

**Key findings:**
1. Humans have only ~20,500 genes (not the >100,000 expected). ~60% of these are similar to fly genes. Only ~2% of human DNA codes for genes.
2. Most genes code for more than one protein through **alternative splicing**. Each of ~20,000 genes has ~5 isoforms → >100,000 proteins. Cells express the same genome but different genes and isoforms.
3. Humans are 99.9% identical. The 0.1% variation = ~3 million differences, mostly SNPs. Most SNPs are outside genes and have no effect on phenotype.
4. Location of >1,400 disease-causing mutations identified (before HGP: <100 known).

### GWAS

- **Genome Wide Association Studies (GWAS)**: identify common genetic variants (SNPs) associated with complex diseases and traits, by testing hundreds of thousands of SNPs in large populations.
- ~10 million SNPs constitute 90% of human genetic variation.
- When certain SNPs appear more frequently in diseased patients than controls, they are said to be associated with the disease.
- Association ≠ certainty. Having an associated SNP increases risk, not guarantees disease.
- **Pharmacogenomics**: how patient genomes affect response to treatment.
- By 2015: >14,000 SNPs significantly associated with >1,500 traits in a public catalog.

### Private Genome Sequencing

- Services like 23andMe (partial genome, ancestry focus, cheaper) and Illumina Clinical Laboratory (whole genome, full advice, more expensive).
- Key considerations: whole genome vs SNP panel; clinical counselling offered; data privacy (especially US companies); results give risk, not certainty for complex diseases.

## Topic 2 — Development and Stem Cells
**Lecturer:** Dr Maggy Fostier

### Part A: Human Development

- A fertilised egg (zygote) develops into trillions of cells with 220 cell types organised in tissues and organs.
- **Cleavage**: the zygote divides by mitosis into blastomeres, partitioning the cytoplasm. At 5 days: blastocyst with inner cell mass (ICM = future embryo) and trophoblast (future placenta).
- **Gastrulation**: coordinated cell movements organise ICM cells into three germ layers:
  - **Ectoderm**: skin and nervous system.
  - **Mesoderm**: muscles, blood, skeleton, connective tissue, heart.
  - **Endoderm**: digestive system, lungs.
- Body axes are set early (anterior-posterior, dorsal-ventral, proximal-distal). The primitive streak defines the AP axis.
- **Neurogenesis** along the AP axis provides the framework. Somites form along the axis → future muscles, vertebral column, dermis.
- **Organogenesis** is progressive: using the head-to-tail framework, cells move, specialise, and organise into tissues and organs.

### Cell Differentiation

- **Potency** decreases as cells specialise: totipotent → pluripotent → multipotent → oligopotent → unipotent → fully differentiated.
- A cell's identity depends on the proteins it expresses, which depends on which genes are switched on.
- At any given time, each cell expresses ~20% of its genes. ~10% of those are developmental genes.
- **Developmental genes** code for transcription factors and signalling proteins — they control which other genes are switched ON or OFF.
- The initial differences between cells come from maternal proteins deposited unevenly in the egg cytoplasm. As cleavage divisions occur, blastomeres end up with different maternal proteins → different gene expression → cascade of further differences.
- **Dolly the sheep (1997)**: proved that a differentiated cell still contains the full genome (genes are not lost during specialisation) but that DNA must be reprogrammed to retrieve totipotency.

### Cell Potency Summary

- Zygote = totipotent (can become a whole organism)
- Inner cell mass = pluripotent (can become any cell in the body)
- Ectoderm/mesoderm/endoderm = multipotent (can become cell types within that lineage)
- Fully differentiated cell = no further division/specialisation

### Part B: Stem Cells

### What is a Stem Cell?

- A stem cell can proliferate (divide) and specialise (differentiate).
- **Telomeres**: non-coding DNA repeats at chromosome ends. Shorten at each mitosis → limit proliferative potential. Cells expressing telomerase protect their telomeres and maintain proliferative capacity.

### Types of Stem Cells

**Embryonic Stem Cells (ESCs):**
- Derived from the inner cell mass.
- Immortal and pluripotent.
- Can make any cell in the body.
- Differentiation controlled in vitro by changing culture medium cocktail.

**Adult Stem Cells (ASCs):**
- Present in many tissues (bone marrow, intestines, fat, skin) but often rare and divide infrequently.
- Multipotent — though some show plasticity in the lab.
- Bone marrow contains haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs → blood) and mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs → connective tissue types: bone, cartilage, tendons, muscle, fat).
- **Plasticity of ASCs**: capacity to trans-differentiate into cells from a different germ layer in the lab (low efficiency). MSCs are the most plastic — can become liver cells (endoderm) and brain cells (ectoderm).

**Neonatal Stem Cells (placenta/umbilical cord blood):**
- Contain HSCs and MSCs.
- Less immunogenic than adult bone marrow.
- Longer telomeres, less DNA damage, non-invasive harvesting.
- Problem: small quantity (though expansion protocols now exist).
- Can be privately stored or donated at birth.

**Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs):**
- Somatic cells (e.g. skin cells) genetically reprogrammed with 4 genes to revert to a pluripotent state resembling ESCs.
- First created November 2007 (Nobel Prize 2012 to Shinya Yamanaka).
- Same protocols as ESCs for differentiation.
- Applications: disease in a dish (model diseases), organ on a chip, autologous cell therapy.

### Immunocompatibility in Transplants

- All cells carry surface proteins (encoded by 5 highly polymorphic genes) recognised as "self" by the immune system.
- Foreign cells without matching surface proteins are attacked.
- **Graft versus host disease**: donor immune cells accidentally attack the recipient.
- Neonatal cells are less immunogenic because foetuses/neonates express fewer surface markers.

### Stem Cell Therapies

**Currently approved:**
- Skin grafts.
- HSC transplant from adult bone marrow or neonatal cells (treats leukaemia and other blood disorders).

**Recent advances:**
- Tissue engineering (e.g. tracheal engineering with patient's own stem cells — autologous transplant, 2008; also bladder and heart valve work).
- MSCs for cartilage repair/regeneration (isolated from bone marrow, fat tissue, or umbilical cord).
- ESC-derived retinal pigmented epithelial (RPE) cells — phase III trials for blindness. Eye is immunologically privileged; few cells needed; internal control with other eye.

**MSCs are particularly promising** because they are easy to isolate and grow in large quantities, can be frozen/thawed, possess immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory effects, and can home to sites of injury.

**iPSC potential:** disease modelling, organ-on-a-chip, personalised autologous therapy.

**Reality check:** Most therapies require three phases of clinical trials, which take a long time. Many foreign clinics advertise unproven MSC treatments with no published trial data — exercise caution.

## Topic 3 — Precision Medicine
**Lecturer:** Dr Shazia Hyatt

### Definitions

- **Personalised Medicine**: preventative care and drug therapy based on the individual's genetic and other relevant information.
- **Precision Medicine**: tailoring medical treatment to individual patient characteristics, usually classifying patients into subpopulations. "The right medicine, at the right time, at the right dose."
- **P4 Medicine**: predictive, preventative, personalised, and participatory medicine.
- **Biomarker**: a measurable indicator of a biological state (e.g. glucose, hormone level, tumour marker, gene variant).

### History

- 1902: Archibald Garrod — inheritance of alkaptonuria.
- 1907: Reuben Ottenberg — first blood transfusion using compatibility testing.
- 1956: genetic basis of primaquine toxicity discovered (G6PD deficiency).
- 1977: CYP450 enzyme variation linked to drug overdose toxicity.
- 2003: Human Genome Project completed — beginning of the real era of personalised medicine.
- At HGP start: 4 drugs with pharmacogenomic information. Now: 261 drugs, 362 drug-biomarker pairs.

### Traditional vs Precision Medicine

- Traditional: "one size fits all" — blockbuster drug for the "typical" patient, not successful for everyone.
- Precision: takes individual differences (genetic, environmental, lifestyle) into account.

### What Data Counts?

- Symptoms, physical exam, medical history.
- Lab tests (blood, urine) and imaging.
- Family history and genetics.
- Lifestyle and environment.
- Omics data: genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, lipidomics, metabolomics, epigenomics.

### Uses of Biomarkers

1. **Diagnosis** (presence of disease): e.g. PSA for prostate cancer. Note: PSA has low specificity — only 25–33% of men with high PSA have cancer.
2. **Risk prediction** (susceptibility): e.g. BRCA1/2 mutations — 85% lifetime chance of breast cancer if positive (vs 13% baseline); 60% lifetime chance of ovarian cancer (vs 0.7% baseline).
3. **Prognosis**: e.g. MammaPrint — 70-gene risk of recurrence signature for early-stage breast cancer.
4. **Predictive** (is a treatment appropriate): e.g. HER2 testing — 25–30% of breast cancer patients are HER2+. Trastuzumab (Herceptin) gives 52% reduction in tumour recurrence compared to chemotherapy alone in HER2+ patients.
5. **Pharmacogenomics (PGx)** (is the drug safe/effective): e.g. CYP450 enzyme variants. CYP450 family metabolises >90% of drugs. Variants lead to slow, normal, fast, or ultra-fast metabolisers. Clopidogrel (Plavix) example: metabolised by CYP2C19; poor metabolisers have 3-fold higher clot risk after stent implantation.
6. **Monitoring treatment response**: e.g. Ki67 — nuclear protein strongly associated with tumour proliferation; reduction in Ki67 indicates treatment is working.

### Application 1: Cancer

- Cancer cells often have DNA changes driving growth. Different tumours can look similar but behave differently.
- Tumour profiling via biopsy: tests can look for gene changes, protein levels, or other markers.
- **EGFR-mutant lung cancer**: EGFR pathway changes drive growth. EGFR inhibitors used if mutation present (e.g. exon 19 deletion, L858R). Resistance can develop over time; re-testing guides next-line therapy.
- **HER2-positive breast cancer**: HER2 overexpression promotes cell growth. Tested via IHC (protein level) and FISH (gene amplification). Trastuzumab improves outcomes in HER2+ patients.
- **Oncotype DX**: 16-gene assay for ER-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer. Calculates recurrence score (1–100) determining need for chemotherapy. Costs ~$4,175.
- **Companion diagnostics (CDx)**: e.g. Cobas platform detects BRAF V600E mutation (real-time PCR) → guides Zelboraf treatment. FDA-approved CDx helps accelerate access to market.

### Application 2: Pharmacogenomics

- PGx = how genes affect how medicines work in the body.
- CYP2C19 & Clopidogrel: Clopidogrel is a prodrug activated by CYP2C19. Loss-of-function variants reduce activation → less platelet inhibition → higher clot risk. Poor metabolisers should use alternative antiplatelet strategy.
- Over 60,000 variations in CYP450 enzymes identified; 90% rare. Each person carries >1,000 gene variations affecting drug response. Current tests only cover a fraction.

### Application 3: Infectious Disease

- Precision in infectious disease = identify the exact pathogen plus its resistance profile.
- Genomic sequencing allows cases to be compared at high resolution to identify transmission chains.
- Used to track SARS-CoV-2 variants; detect hospital outbreaks (e.g. MRSA); monitor emergence of resistance.

### Technologies

- DNA biomarkers: DNA sequencing, PCR, microarrays.
- Protein biomarkers: ELISA, gel electrophoresis, surface plasmon resonance, mass spectrometry, Raman spectroscopy.
- Bundled tests and multiplexing allow multiple biomarkers to be identified from the same sample simultaneously.

### Controversies

- **Ethics**: genome data raises questions about storage, access, potential misuse, and legal protection.
- **Multiple mutations**: a second mutation may render targeted therapy ineffective. Driver vs passenger mutations must be distinguished.
- **Statistics**: 'omics technologies cover thousands of variables; statistical robustness is a concern. AI may help handle data volume.
- **Does it work?** Headlines include "Precision medicine failing to deliver." But also clear success stories (e.g. Dr Lukus Wartman — cancer genome sequenced, gene in "overdrive" identified, targeted treatment given; still in remission 15 years later).

### Other Applications

- Precision devices: tinnitus maskers, quantitative EEG software, artificial pancreas, custom splints.
- 3D printing: polypills with patient-specific dosing (e.g. Spritam for epilepsy); 3D-printed tissues and organs.
- Direct-to-consumer tests: 23andMe (ancestry, carrier status, partial genome).

## Topic 4 — The Human Brain
**Lecturer:** Dr Harry Potter

### Lecture 1: Brain Structure and Function

**Basic anatomy:**
- The brain controls important body functions (walking, sitting, standing, cardiovascular and respiratory) and processes information (dreams, emotions, reasoning).
- Highly convoluted surface: ridges = **gyri**, valleys = **sulci/fissures**. Convolutions allow more cortical surface area.

**Four lobes of the cerebral cortex:**
- **Frontal lobe**: movement, personality, and planning. Contains the primary motor cortex.
- **Parietal lobe**: awareness of surroundings and stereognosis (identifying objects by touch). Contains the primary somatosensory cortex.
- **Temporal lobe**: hearing and language.
- **Occipital lobe**: processing visual signals.

**Other structures:**
- **Cerebellum**: coordinates movement and posture.
- **Brainstem** (midbrain, pons, medulla oblongata): cardiovascular and respiratory centres.

**Nervous system organisation:**
- Central Nervous System (CNS): brain + spinal cord.
- Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): spinal nerves + cranial nerves.
- Somatic nervous system: voluntary movement.

### The Neuron

- Basic unit of the nervous system.
- Signal travels along axon via **saltatory conduction** (jumping between nodes of Ranvier, where myelin gaps are).
- **Neuroglia**: support cells.
  - Schwann cells: myelinate neurons in the PNS.
  - Oligodendrocytes: myelinate neurons in the CNS.
  - Astrocytes: maintain homeostasis, metabolic support, blood-brain barrier.
  - Microglia: resident phagocytes of the brain; also involved in synaptic pruning.

### The Action Potential

- **Resting membrane potential**: –70mV. Inside of the cell is negative. Na+ channels closed; some K+ channels open; Na+/K+ exchanger maintains balance.
- When stimulated: all Na+ channels open. Sodium floods in (down concentration gradient). Membrane **depolarises** (inside becomes positive).
- **Action potential**: an "all or nothing" explosion of electrical activity. The neuron either fires fully or not at all.
- **Repolarisation**: Na+/K+ pump restores resting potential.
- Action potentials travel down the axon, away from the cell body.

### The Synapse

- Gap between neurons = **synaptic cleft**.
- Action potential reaches the axon terminal → vesicles containing neurotransmitters fuse with the pre-synaptic membrane → neurotransmitters released into synaptic cleft → bind to shape-specific receptors on the post-synaptic neuron.
- Lock and key: neurotransmitters (key) must match the receptor (lock).
- Can cause **excitatory** effect (triggers action potential) or **inhibitory** effect (dampens post-synaptic neuron).

### Lecture 2: Risk Factors for Mental Health Disorders

**Nature vs nurture:**
- All human diseases, including mental health disorders, involve a mix of genes and environment (GxE).
- Some disorders have a strong genetic component (e.g. Huntington's disease — entirely caused by the HTT gene).
- But even here, environment shapes onset, progression, symptoms, and treatment response.

**What is a risk factor?**
- Something that increases the chance of developing a disease — not a guarantee.
- Risk factors can be genetic (G) or environmental (E).
- Some confer large risk, some small. A traumatic event can "set off" symptoms, like a glass overflowing. Resilience factors provide extra "space in the glass."

**Environmental risk factors for mental health disorders include:**
- Infections during pregnancy, maternal stress, maternal malnutrition, obstetric complications, low birth weight, growing up in an urban area, cannabis exposure during adolescence, social isolation during adolescence, physical/sexual abuse during childhood, loss of a parent during childhood, low socioeconomic status, paternal age above ~40 or below ~20.

### Developmental Origins of Health and Disease (DOHaD)

- **David Barker's hypothesis**: early-life environmental exposures (nutrition, stress, chemicals, inflammation) during prenatal, perinatal, and neonatal stages permanently shape long-term health and risk for chronic disease, including mental health disorders.
- This matters because we still lack safe and effective treatments for most mental health conditions.

### Maternal Immune Activation (MIA)

- A recognised risk factor for neurodevelopmental disorders (e.g. schizophrenia) in adult offspring.
- **Evidence**: Dutch Hunger Winter (1944–45) and 1957 Influenza A2 pandemic studies — those exposed to flu during their second trimester had elevated risk of schizophrenia.
- Viral, bacterial, and parasitic infections during pregnancy are all linked to schizophrenia in offspring.
- MIA studied in animal models using inflammatory cytokines (chemical messengers of inflammation).

**Three hypotheses for how MIA leads to schizophrenia:**
1. **Placental route**: MIA affects placental function, disrupting fetal development (endocrine, transport, inflammation functions).
2. **Fetal brain route**: maternal cytokines cross into the fetal brain and disrupt fetal brain development (cytokines normally help guide immature neurons to target regions).
3. **Postnatal maternal care route**: MIA impairs maternal care behaviours after birth (placental endocrine function affects onset of maternal care).

**What happens in the MIA offspring brain?**
- **Microglia** become "activated" in schizophrenia and MIA offspring → pathological synaptic pruning (removing too many synapses).
- **Myelination** is disrupted: reduced white matter, decreased oligodendrocyte number, decreased myelin proteins (MBP, OLIG2).
- GWAS evidence supports myelin-related genes as genetic risk factors for schizophrenia.

### Epigenetics

- Genetics = the house blueprint. **Epigenetics** = the contractor telling you when and how many to build.
- The genetic code is fixed; the epigenetic code can be changed AND inherited.
- **Methylation** = an epigenetic mark that controls gene expression.
- MIA causes epigenetic changes in offspring brains (altered methylation) on genes controlling myelination and microglia function.
- This is how our brains "remember" trauma — the environment leaves a lasting mark on gene expression.

## Topic 5 — Microbes and Infectious Disease
**Lecturer:** Dr Ruth Grady

### Types of Microorganism

- **Viruses** (nanometre scale, 10⁻⁹ m): genetic elements that replicate inside cells. Consist of nucleic acid surrounded by protein. Classified by nucleic acid type, replication method, and whether single or double stranded.
- **Bacteria** (micrometre scale, 10⁻⁶ m): single-celled prokaryotes with no membrane-bound nucleus.
- Also: fungi and parasites.

### General Concepts

- **Koch's postulates**: the framework for establishing that a specific microorganism causes a specific disease.

### Emerging and Re-emerging Infectious Diseases

Types of emerging/re-emerging infections:
1. **Old diseases now known to be microbiological** — e.g. stomach ulcers caused by *Helicobacter pylori*; cervical cancer caused by HPV.
2. **Old infections re-emerging due to drug resistance** — e.g. XDR-TB (extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis), MRSA.
3. **Diseases thought eradicated but re-emerged** — e.g. syphilis, chlamydia (linked to changes in sexual behaviour and contraception use).
4. **Recognised infections spreading to new populations** — e.g. Zika, Ebola, malaria and dengue moving north due to climate change.
5. **New infections from changes in existing microorganisms** — e.g. bird/swine flu (influenza RNA viruses can reassort — antigenic shift — when different strains infect the same animal); SARS, SARS-CoV-2.
6. **Discovering new roles for bacteria** — e.g. gut microbiome in health and disease.

### Antibiotics

**How antibiotics work:**
- Inhibit cell wall formation (e.g. penicillin, ceftriaxone).
- Inhibit protein synthesis (e.g. tetracycline, erythromycin, azithromycin, spectinomycin).
- Several other mechanisms.

**Why is investing in new antibiotics unattractive commercially?** Short treatment courses = low revenue; resistance can undermine new drugs quickly.

### Antibiotic Resistance

**Mechanisms of resistance:**
1. **Drug impermeability** (innate): bacteria have an outer membrane that antibiotics cannot penetrate.
2. **Drug efflux**: bacteria pump antibiotics out of the cell.
3. **Target modification**: bacteria alter the target so the antibiotic can no longer bind.
4. **Drug inactivation**: bacteria produce enzymes that destroy the antibiotic (e.g. beta-lactamases destroy penicillins).
5. **Target bypass**: bacteria use an alternative pathway that bypasses the antibiotic's target.

One antibiotic class can be overcome by different mechanisms; different antibiotic classes can share the same resistance mechanism.

**Why does resistance arise?**
- Bacteria acquire resistance genes from other bacteria via horizontal gene transfer (plasmid DNA — extra-chromosomal, mobile).
- Bacteria grow quickly → mutations accumulate quickly.
- Overuse of antibiotics (over-the-counter in some countries, animal husbandry).
- Patients stopping antibiotics too soon before the infection is cleared.

**New approaches:**
- Historical: natural compounds, semi-synthetic/synthetic drugs.
- Genetic era: genome analysis to identify new targets.
- Post-genome era: screening libraries of older compounds, systems biology, AI for identifying potential proteins/motifs.
- **Quorum sensing (QS)**: bacteria communicate using small signalling molecules ("autoinducers") that bind receptors on neighbouring bacteria, altering gene expression (e.g. toxin production, flagella). Quorum quenching — interfering with QS signals — is a potential new antibacterial strategy.

### Phage Therapy

- **Bacteriophages**: viruses that infect bacteria. Estimated 10³¹ on Earth. Discovered 1915 and 1917 independently.
- Lytic phages kill bacteria; lysogenic phages enter dormancy (latency) in the host.
- Highly specific to a strain of a bacterial species — this is both an advantage and a disadvantage.
- Used in the 1930s but largely superseded by antibiotics. In the Soviet bloc, cocktails of phage preparations were used therapeutically.
- Antibiotic resistance has revived interest in phage therapy.

**Pros:**
- Multiply in hosts then are naturally cleared.
- Can avoid killing normal microflora.
- Cocktails may reduce resistance.
- Low dosing levels; can disrupt biofilms.

**Cons:**
- Immunogenic — cleared from blood; may only be useful locally.
- Limited knowledge of phage biology.
- Latency risk; toxin release; strain variation; resistance can still emerge; IP/patent issues.
- FDA licensed for food processing (against *Listeria*) in 2006. Human use: compassionate use only when antibiotics have failed.

### Healthcare-Associated Infections (HAIs)

- **MRSA** (*Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus*):
  - *S. aureus* is a skin and throat commensal.
  - First reported 1961. At peak: ~7,000 new MRSA blood infection cases per year in UK; now ~700 (76% reduction since 2007).
- **Pseudomonas aeruginosa**: lung infections (especially cystic fibrosis); naturally resistant to many antibiotics; forms biofilms.
- ***Clostridioides difficile*** (C. diff): diarrhoeal infection in ill patients. Spores not killed by alcohol hand gel. At peak (2007): 55,498 cases; now ~18,970 (69% reduction).
- **Carbapenemase-Producing Enterobacteriaceae (CPE)**: resistant to many antibiotics; found in ~25% of healthy people as gut commensals; cause serious infections.

### Faecal Microbiota Transplants (FMT)

- Transfer of microbiota from a healthy donor to a patient.
- **85% cure rate** for chronic *C. difficile* infections.
- Not fully defined what a "healthy" microbiome is — may vary between individuals; the key factor may be metabolites rather than bacteria themselves.
- **Autologous** (patient's own, stored before illness) vs **heterologous** (donor).
- Risks: could transmit depression, anxiety, obesity, or other conditions linked to the microbiome.
- Ethical implications: microflora can be passed on — informed consent? Can they be passed to offspring?
- Key paper: Larsen & Brummer (2024), *Frontiers in Microbiology*, 15, 1449133.

### Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)

**Common STIs in the UK:** chlamydia, gonorrhoea, syphilis, HIV, herpes, HPV.

**Chlamydia** (*Chlamydia trachomatis*):
- Most commonly diagnosed STI in England: >200,000 cases annually.
- Gram-negative obligate intracellular bacteria.
- Often asymptomatic (50% of men; 70–80% of women).
- Symptoms: pain on urination, unusual discharge, bleeding between periods (women).

**Gonorrhoea** (*Neisseria gonorrhoeae*, "the clap"):
- Gram-negative cocci; adhere to and invade epithelial cells of urethra/cervix.
- Symptoms: pain on urination, thick yellow-green discharge; can cause pelvic inflammatory disease, infertility.
- **Resistance**: 2018 — first confirmed case of extensively drug-resistant *N. gonorrhoeae* globally (England; acquired in Asia). Resistant to azithromycin and ceftriaxone; only susceptible to spectinomycin. Cases increasing: 2015–2021: 9 UK cases; 2022–2024: 15 cases (5 extensively drug-resistant).

**Syphilis** (*Treponema pallidum*):
- Unusual spirochaete shape and cell wall. Hard to grow in the lab.
- Three/four stages: primary (painless chancre), secondary (hypersensitive rash), latent, tertiary (neurological/systemic complications, in ~15% of untreated cases).
- 2023: 8,195 new diagnoses in England — highest since 1948. ~900 women tested positive during pregnancy in 2022.
- Rise linked to changes in sexual behaviour and contraception (e.g. uptake of hormonal contraception and PrEP reducing condom use).

## Topic 6 — Conservation Biology
**Lecturer:** Dr Thomas Nuhse

### What is Biodiversity?

Biological diversity = the variability among living organisms from all sources (terrestrial, marine, aquatic ecosystems and their ecological complexes), including diversity:

**Three levels:**
1. **Genetic diversity** (within-species): diversity of genes/alleles within a population. Also heritable variation across different populations of the same species. Genetic diversity is the raw material of evolution. Measured using DNA-based techniques.
2. **Species diversity** (between-species): variety and abundance of different organisms in an area. Two components: species richness (number of different species in an area) and species evenness (relative abundance of each species).
3. **Ecosystem diversity**: variety of habitats in a region or the mosaic of patches in a landscape.

**Genetic diversity example**: *Heliconius melpomene* butterfly — geographic variants of a single species showing huge phenotypic variation in wing patterns, controlled by surprisingly few genes. Different species in the same location often resemble each other (Müllerian mimicry — all unpalatable to predators).

**Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG)**: part of the Environment Act (2021) — most medium and large UK developments must measurably increase site biodiversity by 10%.

### Biodiversity Loss and Extinction

**Main drivers of biodiversity loss:**
1. Deforestation and habitat degradation (habitat loss).
2. Pollution.
3. Climate change.
4. Overexploitation.
5. Invasive species.

**Key concept — extinction vortex**: small populations enter positive feedback loops of inbreeding, reduced genetic variation, and further population decline. Loss of genetic variation is the key factor in extinction.

**Species most vulnerable to extinction:**
- Low population density, found in small area, specialised niche, low reproductive rate.
- Endemic species (exist only in one geographic region) are particularly vulnerable.

**Species least likely to become extinct:**
- High population density, found over a large area, generalised niche, high reproductive rate.

**Case study**: Variable harlequin toad (*Atelopus varius*) — critically endangered due to habitat destruction, climate change, and chytrid fungal disease (Bd, Bsal). Likely only one population left in Panama. Manchester Museum has a captive breeding programme.

### Conservation Strategies

- Prioritise areas for conservation (using hotspots, keystone/umbrella species).
- Restore damaged areas.
- Debt-for-nature swaps.
- Ecosystem valuation.
- Captive breeding programmes.
- Re-introductions.
- Reducing human footprint.
- In-situ conservation (protecting patches with high genetic variability).
- Ex-situ conservation (preserving germplasm/tissue in seed banks).

**Hotspots** (Conservation International definition): must have at least 1,500 endemic vascular plant species AND 30% or less of original natural vegetation remaining. 36 hotspots worldwide covering just 2.5% of Earth's land surface but supporting >50% of the world's plant species as endemics and ~43% of bird, mammal, reptile, and amphibian species as endemics.

**Keystone species**: disproportionately important to the ecosystem — removing them drastically changes or destroys it. e.g. Almendro tree (*Dipteryx panamensis*) in Costa Rica — 88% of Great Green Macaw nest sites are in this tree; many animals depend on its seeds.

**Umbrella species**: conservation of these species indirectly conserves many others in the ecosystem. Value tied to geographic species range. e.g. Great Green Macaw (*Ara ambigua*) as umbrella species for the Almendro forest habitat.

### Invasive Alien Species

- **Native species**: have co-evolved with many other species in their ecosystem over a long time.
- **Non-native/alien/exotic species**: live outside their native range due to human introduction. If they establish: naturalised.
- **Invasive species**: alien species that spread and cause harm to their environment.

**How invasives cause harm:**
- Disease vectors and competitors (grey squirrels transmit squirrelpox to red squirrels; harlequin ladybugs transmit microsporidian parasites to native ladybirds; signal crayfish transmit crayfish plague).
- Aggressive predators (American mink caused 95% decline of UK water voles; feral cats responsible for ~20 mammal extinctions in Australia).
- Economic damage (floating pennywort smothers rivers; rabbits cause £170m annual crop losses in the UK; Japanese knotweed — eradication legally required, costs £250m annually).

**Why invasives are a growing threat**: increased global movement of goods and people, land degradation, trade in exotic pets and houseplants, climate change.

**The UK "big four" Victorian introductions from Asia:**
1. Giant hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*) — phototoxic sap causes severe burns.
2. Japanese knotweed (*Fallopia japonica*) — extreme structural damage; herbicide legally required.
3. Himalayan balsam (*Impatiens glandulifera*) — dominates riverbanks; explosive seedpods; destabilises river banks.
4. Rhododendron.

**What makes a non-native species invasive? Five concept clusters (Enders et al., 2020):**
1. **Trait cluster**: short generation times, many offspring (r-strategists), better adaptability (generalists), "novel weapons."
2. **Darwin's cluster**: new environment is more susceptible (e.g. islands), species is more/less related to established species, finds an empty ecological niche.
3. **Biotic interaction cluster**: escapes natural parasites, pathogens, and predators not present in new environment. Plants allocate more carbon to growth than defence.
4. **Propagule cluster**: introduced in large numbers and/or often; previously introduced aliens "pave the way."
5. **Resource availability cluster**: new environment temporarily richer in resources from disturbance.

**Management approaches:**
- Trapping and killing (e.g. coypu eradicated from SE England 1960–1989 — no sightings since).
- Pesticides/herbicides (glyphosate for Japanese knotweed; "balsam bashing" for Himalayan balsam — effective short-term only).
- **Biological control** — using natural enemies. Must be used with caution: cane toads introduced to Australia in 1935 devastated native reptiles and quolls; harlequin ladybug introduced to Europe as biocontrol for aphids, now one of the world's most invasive insects. Current research: knotweed psyllids (*Aphalara itadori*), leaf-spot fungus (*Mycosphaerella polygoni-cuspidati*) as mycoherbicide.
- Monitoring and prevention: Great Britain Non-native Species Secretariat (NNSS); CABI Invasive Species Compendium.

**Debate**: Is concern over non-native species xenophobic? Counter-argument: conservation biologists oppose only those species threatening ecosystems, habitats, or other species; severe long-term harm can take decades to manifest and should not be dismissed as harmless prematurely.
